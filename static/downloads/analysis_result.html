Later, during the hushed concentration of the composition exam, a choked sob sliced through the quiet. It was Liam, his shoulders shaking subtly beneath his desk. The image of his suppressed grief, a silent scream trapped within the confines of the exam hall, seared itself onto my memory. It was a visceral, shameful tableau.

He left a week later, transferred to another school, swallowed by the anonymity of a new environment. News of him faded, but the sound of his stifled weeping remains – a persistent, haunting echo in the chambers of my conscience. It wasn't the cruelty of the bullies that stays with me, but my own cowardice, my failure to extend a hand, a word, a simple act of solidarity. Liam’s silent tears became a testament to my own complicity, a stark reminder of the betrayal of unspoken support.

That haunting sob is my constant companion now, a visceral alarm bell that rings whenever I witness injustice. It compels me to act, to intervene, to break the silence, to ensure that no one else suffers the isolating agony of unheard cries. The memory is painful, but it is also a powerful catalyst for change, a grim lesson learned too late, but hopefully not in vain.
